# 1.6 Ethical, Legal, Cultural and Environmental Impacts of Digital Technology

> **Component:** J277/01 – Computer Systems | **Section:** 1.6 | **Exam:** J277/01

---

## What you need to know

Computing affects society in many ways – not just technically, but ethically, legally, culturally, and environmentally. You need to be able to discuss different viewpoints, apply relevant UK legislation, and evaluate the impact of technology on individuals and society. This topic often appears in extended-response questions.

## Key terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Ethics | Moral principles that guide behaviour; what is right or wrong |
| Privacy | The right of individuals to control how their personal data is collected and used |
| Digital divide | The gap between those who have access to digital technology and those who do not |
| E-waste | Discarded electronic devices and components |
| Computer Misuse Act 1990 | UK law making it illegal to access, modify, or use a computer system without authorisation |

## Core notes

### Ethical issues

**Privacy**
- Digital technology collects vast amounts of personal data (browsing history, location, purchases, communications).
- Ethical debate: where does useful data collection end and privacy invasion begin?
- Examples: social media profiling, targeted advertising, smart home devices always listening.

**AI and algorithmic bias**
- AI systems trained on biased data can produce biased outcomes (e.g. facial recognition performing worse for darker skin tones; hiring algorithms favouring certain demographics).
- Raises questions about fairness and accountability.

**Surveillance**
- CCTV, employee monitoring, government surveillance.
- Balance between security (legitimate surveillance) and civil liberties (right to privacy).

**Access to technology**
- Not everyone can access the internet or digital devices equally.
- Raises ethical questions about fairness, education, and opportunity.

---

### Legal issues

**Computer Misuse Act 1990**

| Offence | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Unauthorised access to computer material | Hacking into someone's account |
| Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences | Hacking a bank to commit fraud |
| Unauthorised modification of computer material | Releasing a virus; deleting another person's files |
| Making, supplying, or obtaining articles for use in offences | Creating hacking tools |

**Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR**

The DPA 2018 implements UK GDPR. Key principles:
- Data must be processed **lawfully, fairly, and transparently**.
- Data must be collected for **specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes**.
- Data must be **accurate** and kept **up to date**.
- Data must not be kept **longer than necessary**.
- Data must be kept **secure**.
- Individuals have rights: right to access their data, right to erasure ("right to be forgotten"), right to correct inaccurate data.

**Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988**
- Protects creators' intellectual property automatically from the moment of creation.
- Illegal to: copy, share, or distribute someone else's work without permission.
- Applies to: software, music, images, text, videos.
- **Open source** software is an exception – the creator grants permission to use/modify.

### Cultural issues

**Digital divide**
- The gap between those with reliable internet access and digital skills, and those without.
- Causes: wealth, geography (rural vs urban), age, disability.
- Effects: unequal access to education, job opportunities, government services, and social connection.

**Changing work patterns**
- Automation and AI replacing some jobs (manufacturing, data entry, some professional roles).
- New jobs created in technology sectors.
- Remote working / hybrid working enabled by digital technology.
- Gig economy (e.g. food delivery apps, freelance platforms).

**Online communities and social media**
- Positive: connection, support, political organisation, spread of information.
- Negative: spread of misinformation, cyberbullying, echo chambers, addiction.

---

### Environmental issues

**Energy use**
- Data centres consume enormous amounts of electricity.
- Cryptocurrency mining is particularly energy-intensive.
- Network infrastructure requires continuous power.

**E-waste**
- Discarded devices contain toxic materials (lead, mercury, cadmium).
- Improper disposal pollutes soil and water.
- Planned obsolescence encourages frequent device replacement.

**Carbon footprint**
- Manufacturing new devices releases CO₂.
- Some companies commit to renewable energy for data centres (e.g. Google, Microsoft).
- Offsetting schemes and energy efficiency improvements help.

**Reducing environmental impact**
- Buying refurbished devices.
- Proper recycling of e-waste.
- Using energy-efficient hardware.
- Cloud computing can centralise energy use in more efficient data centres.

---

### Privacy issues

- **Personal data collection:** websites, apps, and devices collect data with or without clear consent.
- **Cookies and tracking:** advertising networks track browsing behaviour across websites.
- **Smart devices:** smart speakers, smartwatches, and connected cameras collect continuous data.
- **Social media:** platforms use personal data to target advertising.
- **Data breaches:** large collections of personal data are attractive targets for attackers.

## Worked examples

**Example 1 – Applying the Data Protection Act**

A school stores student medical records.

| DPA principle | How it applies |
|--------------|----------------|
| Lawful and transparent | Parents must be told what data is held |
| Purpose limitation | Medical data can only be used for medical/welfare purposes |
| Accuracy | Records must be updated as health situations change |
| Storage limitation | Records should be deleted after the student leaves (after a defined period) |
| Security | Records must be password-protected and not shared unnecessarily |

---

**Example 2 – Evaluating the digital divide**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Students without home internet | Cannot complete online homework; disadvantaged academically |
| Rural areas with poor broadband | Cannot access telemedicine, online banking, government services |
| Elderly people unfamiliar with technology | Excluded from digital-only services |

## Common mistakes

- ❌ Confusing **legal** with **ethical** – something can be legal but still ethically questionable (e.g. legal data harvesting that feels invasive).
- ❌ Quoting the wrong law – the **Computer Misuse Act** covers hacking; the **Data Protection Act** covers personal data; the **Copyright Act** covers intellectual property.
- ❌ Writing vague answers in extended-response questions – always link your point to a specific example or consequence.
- ❌ Forgetting to give **both sides** in a *discuss* or *evaluate* question.
- ❌ Describing only negative environmental impacts and forgetting that technology can also have positive environmental effects (e.g. remote working reducing commuting emissions).

---

## Exam focus

- **Define** – digital divide, e-waste, intellectual property, open source.
- **Describe** – the key provisions of each piece of legislation.
- **Explain** – why a specific law applies to a given scenario.
- **Discuss** – the impact of a technology on society (expect both positive and negative points).
- **Evaluate** – whether the benefits of a technology outweigh its environmental or ethical costs.

> Extended-response questions (6 marks) in this topic often ask you to *discuss* or *evaluate*. You must present multiple viewpoints and reach a conclusion to access the highest marks.

## Quick-check questions

1. What does the Computer Misuse Act 1990 make illegal?
2. State **two** principles of the Data Protection Act 2018.
3. What is meant by the *digital divide*?
4. Give **two** examples of environmental impacts of digital technology.
5. What is the difference between open-source and proprietary software in terms of the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Unauthorised access to computer material; unauthorised access with intent to commit further crimes; unauthorised modification of computer material (any two of these three main offences).
2. Any two from: data must be processed lawfully and fairly; data must be collected for specified purposes; data must be accurate; data must not be kept longer than necessary; data must be kept secure; data subjects have rights to access and correct their data.
3. The digital divide is the gap between individuals or groups who have access to digital technology and the internet and those who do not, often linked to wealth, geography, age, or disability.
4. Any two: energy consumption of data centres / devices; e-waste from discarded electronics; carbon emissions from manufacturing and data centres; rare earth mining for device components.
5. Under the Copyright Act, software is automatically protected — you cannot copy or distribute it without permission. Open-source software is released with a licence that grants permission to view, modify, and distribute the source code; proprietary software does not grant these permissions.

</details>

## Exam-style practice

**Q1.** A person guesses a friend's password and logs into their social media account without permission. State which law has been broken and identify the specific offence. [2 marks]

*[Mark scheme: Computer Misuse Act 1990 (1); unauthorised access to computer material (1).]*

---

**Q2.** Describe **two** principles of the Data Protection Act 2018 that a hospital must follow when storing patient records. [4 marks]

*[Mark scheme: Any two principles, one mark for naming/describing the principle and one mark for applying it to the hospital context. E.g. Data must be kept secure (1) — the hospital must use password protection and encryption to prevent unauthorised access to patient records (1). Data must not be kept longer than necessary (1) — patient records should be deleted after the patient no longer requires them / after a legally defined retention period (1).]*

---

**Q3.** Discuss the environmental impact of increasing use of cloud computing and streaming services. [6 marks]

*[Mark scheme: Award marks for relevant points, presented from more than one perspective, with a conclusion. Negative: data centres consume large amounts of electricity / generate carbon emissions (1); e-waste from servers and devices (1); cooling systems in data centres use significant energy/water (1). Positive: more energy-efficient to centralise computing in modern data centres than individual users running their own servers (1); remote working enabled by cloud reduces commuting emissions (1); some major providers powered by renewable energy (1). Conclusion: overall impact depends on energy sources and consumption patterns — up to 1 mark for a reasoned conclusion. Max 6.]*
